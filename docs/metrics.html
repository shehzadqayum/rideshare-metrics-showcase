Trip-selection metrics (CNHR / MNHR)

The centre of the suite: a pipeline that turns screenshots of ride offers into an honest hourly rate, so “should I take this trip?” has a numerical answer.

The problem, from scratch. When an offer appears on a driver's screen it shows a headline fare — say £12 for 25 minutes. That number is misleading in three ways: it ignores the unpaid drive to the pickup, it ignores the fixed weekly costs of running the vehicle (insurance, hire, charging — about £430/week here), and it says nothing about whether the week as a whole is on track.

The fix — two metrics. CNHR (cost-normalised hourly rate) is the week's running answer to “after all costs, what has each hour actually paid so far?”. MNHR (marginal net hourly rate) asks the forward-looking question: “at the current pace, is the next hour worth working?”. Both come in a paid variant (paid time only) and a true variant that also counts the unpaid en-route minutes the routes page makes visible. A week is judged against a target rate of £15/h after costs.

How the data gets here. An AI vision model (Claude) reads each screenshot; GPS logs anchor it in space; browser automation supplies the final settled earnings from the portal (tips and adjustments included); parsed charging invoices supply the cost side. The pipeline then computes per-trip and weekly metrics and classifies each week into one of four states.

Cost-normalised hourly rate by week (£/h)

Dot colour = the week's diagnostic state. The dashed line is the £15/h target.
Reading the states. Sustained: above target and holding. Accel recovery: below target but climbing (typical early in a week, before fixed costs are covered). Decelerating: above water but slipping. Stalled: below target and flat. The first week reads −£182/h — six trips against a full week of fixed costs — which is exactly what the metric should say.

The headline finding: regression across these weeks showed weekly hours, not trip quality, set the ceiling on profitability — the binding constraint was time worked, not cleverness of selection. That result reshaped the working pattern itself.
