Driving London, measured

Between December 2025 and April 2026 I drove 890+ private-hire trips across London in an electric vehicle. Alongside the driving I built a suite of software tools — using AI-assisted development with Claude Code — to automate the paperwork and to answer two questions with data: which trip offers are worth accepting, and when is it most productive to work. This site shows the tools and their real output. Nothing here is mocked.

New here? Start with the story. A ride-share driver constantly makes two decisions: accept or decline the next offer, and start or stop the shift. Most drivers decide by feel. These tools decide by measurement — every trip is captured, GPS-tracked, priced against costs, and scored; London's transport network is monitored for the disruptions that create demand; and every pound of charging cost is parsed from invoices back into the equation. The pages above walk through each layer.

The framework behind it

CNHR–MNHR is the measurement framework the whole showcase rests on. A ride-share offer shows a headline fare — say £12 for 25 minutes, apparently £28/hour — but that ignores the unpaid drive to the pickup, the fixed weekly cost of running the vehicle, and whether the week is on track. Two metrics fix it:

CNHR (cost-normalised hourly rate, rₙ = (Eₙ − C_w) / Hₙ) is backward-looking — after the week's fixed £430 of costs, what has each hour actually paid? MNHR (marginal net hourly rate, m = ρ − λ, EMA-smoothed) is forward-looking — is the next hour still worth working? Together they place the week on a four-state diagnostic: sustained, accelerating recovery, decelerating, or stalled.

The headline empirical result: weekly hours, not trip quality, set the ceiling on profitability — because the fixed cost is spread across whatever hours you work.

The tool suite

Routes covered
uber_screen · Python · Leaflet
Every GPS-tracked journey on one map — filter by day, colour by earnings, speed or service, hover any segment for its numbers.
Trip-selection metrics
uber_screen · CNHR/MNHR pipeline
Screenshots of ride offers are read by AI vision, reconciled against the portal, and scored with a cost-normalised hourly rate. Explained from first principles.
Earnings & portal automation
uber · Node.js
Browser automation keeps a complete local record of trips and statements — 17 weeks of weekly earnings, audited.
Demand & shift timing
uber_surge · Sentinel daemon
TfL, rail, fixtures, events and weather scored into an hourly surge forecast with ranked windows and a positioning plan — plus the live feeds it's built from.
EV running costs
uber_charging · Python
Charging invoices from four networks parsed by AI into per-session records — the cost side of every profitability number on this site.
Original dashboards
generated by the pipeline itself
The CNHR–MNHR dashboard the pipeline produces — week selector, break-even markers, per-trip cumulative curves, and a per-week route map (open by default). A single-week deep dive is at the week of 9 Feb 2026.
