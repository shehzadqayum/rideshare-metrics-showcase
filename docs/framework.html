The CNHR–MNHR framework

A formal way to answer the two questions a ride-share driver actually faces: is this trip worth taking? and is this shift still worth working? — derived from 890+ London trips and applied throughout this showcase.

The problem it solves. When an offer appears it shows a headline fare — say £12 for 25 minutes, which looks like £28/hour. That number is misleading three ways: it ignores the unpaid drive to the pickup, it ignores the fixed weekly cost of running the vehicle, and it says nothing about whether the week as a whole is on track. CNHR and MNHR fix each of those in turn.

The two metrics

CNHR — Cost-Normalised Hourly Rate rₙ

Backward-looking: “what has each hour actually paid so far?”
rₙ = (Eₙ − C_w) / Hₙ

Cumulative earnings minus the whole week's fixed cost, divided by hours worked. It starts deeply negative (you owe the week's costs before you've earned anything) and climbs as the week progresses. It answers “am I actually ahead?”

MNHR — Marginal Net Hourly Rate m̃ₙ

Forward-looking: “is the next hour worth working?”
m = ρ − λ  ·  m̃ₙ = α·m + (1−α)·m̃ₙ₋₁

The rate a trip earns above the cost of the time it consumes, smoothed with an exponential moving average so one good or bad trip doesn't swing the signal. It answers “is the current pace still paying?”

Why both. CNHR alone tells you where you stand but not where you're heading; MNHR alone tells you the trend but not whether costs are covered. Read together they give a position and a direction — which is exactly the four-state diagnostic below.

The four-state diagnostic

Position (CNHR) × direction (MNHR trend). This is the signal the dashboards colour-code each week by.
Improving — m̃_true > rₙ
Deteriorating — m̃_true ≤ rₙ
Above target
rₙ ≥ r*
SUSTAINED Above target and still pulling up. Keep doing exactly this.
DECELERATING Above target but the marginal rate is dropping — the good position is eroding.
Below target
rₙ < r*
ACCEL RECOVERY Behind, but recent trips beat the average — momentum is closing the gap.
STALLED Behind and not improving. Change something: area, hours, or trip selection.
The value of the 2×2 is that a below-target week isn't automatically bad — ACCEL RECOVERY says the week is being rescued, while DECELERATING warns that a comfortable-looking week is quietly slipping.

What the data showed

Across 17 weeks the binding constraint on profitability turned out to be weekly hours, not trip quality. Because C_w is fixed, CNHR is dominated by the denominator Hₙ — working more hours spreads the fixed cost further and lifts the rate far more reliably than cherry-picking better-paying trips. That single finding reshaped the working pattern.
